Tara De La Garza Interview

by Lily Sullivan

In this interview with Tara de la Garza, the curator and artist discusses her curation of At Stake in Spring Break Art Fair, along with her works and inspirations. De La Garza incorporates both science and the occult in her curation. Topics covered in this interview include her zero waste studio efforts and collective push against consumer culture.

Your sculpture "Basketball Pillar" uses cement, a basketball, and plastic. What inspires your use of unique mixed media? How long would a sculpture like this take to create?

My work pushes back against consumer culture. I endeavor to have a zero-waste studio and to create projects that encourage minimizing collective consumption. I create problems for myself by collecting materials and challenge myself to use them. During the pandemic, our studio program became a ghost town. One day an old basketball rolled out in front of my door. It stayed, and became part of the fabric. The sculptures take anywhere between a few days and a few weeks to make, depending on materials. I use pillar formworks for some pieces and pack them with Styrofoam, poured cement and hard plastics on the periphery for texture and interest. I call this method ‘sequestering’ and they essentially become time capsules of consumption. They can be interpreted as a simulacra of decay, the totemic shapes often returning to a state rendered organic, monuments to an era that may become to be known as The Plastocene.

What motivated your collaboration with artists Joseph Ari Aloi and Leslie Lanxinger in At Stake?

Joseph Ari Aloi and I first worked together in 2009, during another economic downturn caused by the financial crisis. I was working with Manon Slome, building “No Longer Empty”, an organization that created massive installations in empty storefronts in NYC. Joseph was one of the artists we curated into an exhibition at the Chelsea Hotel. He spent days laboriously drawing on multiple surfaces using his intricate calligraphy style. I was exhibiting defunct neon signs ‘collaged’ together and curating a section of the show. We kept in touch and, especially through the pandemic, began a visual dialogue, collaborating on our version of exquisite corpses and triptychs exhibited at Spring/Break LA.

Surviving as humans during climate change will require adapting. Humans have evolved through natural selection, constantly going through a process of adaptation, destruction and rebuilding as part of the human experience. How we coexist with disaster in the future will involve enormous amounts of human ingenuity; how do we keep flood/fire/famine/war from completely undermining civilization. We touch on some of these ideas in the collaboration asking - what is at stake and what is salvageable. In this installation Joseph Ari Aloi and I worked on and beside each other’s work, riffing in a visual jam session to create our joint projects. We engaged in a version of the 18th century surrealist parlor game, The Exquisite Corpse, Aloi building intricate drawing scaffoldings for the evolved creatures that I create with collaged elements borrowed from nature. These remind us of the interconnected ecosystem we are a part of. The work continues in the form of collaborative triptychs. The triptych was a well-established format for religious paintings in the Middle Ages. The triptych folds in on itself, becoming mobile. Our presentation of this ancient dialogue utilizes frames that are scorched using a Japanese preservation technique called Yaki Sugi. A future necessity in fire zones, the transportable artworks remind us of loss.
